# What is the sweetspot to share? The Result Card Dilemma

The result card sharing after playthrough is the core of our viral loop. It's the thing players walk away with and (ideally) share. My first bet was that people would want to share the specific, personal story of their playthrough. <Green>That was my own inference, and it was wrong</Green>: from 10+ user interviews, the card read as too specific and too vulnerable to put on a feed. So I tried a gradual reveal, letting players assemble their own card piece by piece and choose what to show. Users responded to it, but the founding engineer flagged it as too much engineering overhead against a live launch deadline. The version that shipped reconciled both constraints: <Green>a set of collectible archetypes paired with a short one-liner</Green>, light enough to build and abstract enough that people actually wanted to share it.

On feedback, I worked in a small, high-trust team, so the loop was fast and I had to prioritize without constant direction. When I pitched my product flowchart to the founding engineer, the real conversation was scope: what was worth building against a live launch deadline and what wasn't. Instead of defending my full vision, <Green>I treated his constraints as part of the design problem</Green> and reworked the plan around what we could actually ship. With users, I ran interviews with 10+ participants and let what they told me override what I assumed, translating their reactions straight into the work, even when it meant cutting things I liked.

What Trove taught me is that owning something end-to-end isn't about working alone. <Green>It's about holding the brand and the vision steady</Green> while staying genuinely open to the engineer who says it's too expensive and the user who says it doesn't land, and being willing to move because of both.
